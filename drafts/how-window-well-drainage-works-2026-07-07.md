# How Window Well Drainage Systems Actually Work

Pour a five-gallon bucket of water into your window well and time how long it takes to disappear. If it's still sitting there a minute later, you already know more about your drainage system than most homeowners do, because you just watched it fail.

So how does window well drainage work when it's actually working? It's a simple gravity system: rain falls into the well, filters through a gravel bed, and either drains through a pipe tied into your foundation's drainage system or soaks slowly into the surrounding soil. Every part has to do its job, and if even one piece is missing or clogged, water backs up against your basement window instead of moving away from it.

## The Three Ways Window Wells Actually Drain in Lincoln

Not every window well drains the same way, and knowing which type you have changes how you troubleshoot it.

**Tied to the foundation drain system.** This is the setup you want. A perforated pipe at the bottom of the well connects underground to the same drain tile that runs around your footing, carrying water away from the house to daylight or a sump pit. This is standard on newer construction and any egress window we install today.

**Draining to a standalone dry well.** Some homes route the window well drain to its own gravel-filled pit a few feet away instead of tying into the foundation system. It works on the same gravity principle but doesn't depend on the rest of your foundation drainage being in good shape.

**No drain at all.** A lot of older Lincoln homes, especially those built before the 1990s, have window wells that were never connected to anything. The gravel bed was supposed to let water percolate into the surrounding soil on its own. That works fine for a while, until the soil around it compacts or clay content in the yard slows percolation to a crawl, and then every rain becomes a mini-flood.

If you're not sure which type you have, dig a few inches into the gravel at the bottom of your well. A visible pipe opening means you've got a connected system. Nothing there means you're relying on percolation alone, and that's worth knowing before the next big storm.

## How Water Moves Through the System, Step by Step

Here's the sequence in a window well drainage system that's working the way it should.

Rain hits the well and starts collecting at the bottom, where a layer of gravel, usually 6 to 12 inches deep, sits above the drain opening or the soil. The gravel does two jobs: it slows the water down so it doesn't just pool on top of hard-packed dirt, and it filters out the fine silt and debris that would otherwise clog a drain pipe.

Water works its way down through the gravel and reaches either the drain opening or the bare soil underneath. If there's a pipe, gravity pulls the water into it, and it travels underground until it connects with your home's foundation drain tile or empties into a dry well a short distance away. From there it either flows to daylight somewhere on a slope, or gets picked up by a sump pump and pushed out through a discharge line.

If there's no pipe, the water simply has to soak into the ground faster than it's arriving. That's the weak link. A hard summer downpour can drop more water into a well in ten minutes than average soil can absorb in an hour, which is exactly why percolation-only wells are the ones that flood first.

The whole system depends on grading working with it, not against it. Soil around the foundation should slope away from the house, roughly an inch of drop for every foot out for the first several feet. When that slope is correct, less rainwater even reaches the well in the first place, which takes pressure off everything downstream.

## Why Drainage Systems Fail Over Time

Even a well-built drainage system doesn't last forever without some attention.

Gravel migrates and silts in gradually, especially in wells near trees, and that fine sediment is what actually clogs a drain pipe rather than leaves alone. Nebraska's freeze-thaw cycle is hard on everything underground, expanding standing water in winter and sometimes shifting or cracking pipe connections you can't see without digging. Grading settles over years too, and a slope that carried water away from your foundation a decade ago can flatten out or even reverse without anyone noticing until a storm makes it obvious.

## When to Call a Professional

Some drainage work is genuinely a weekend project. Clearing a clogged drain opening, regrading a few feet of soil, or replacing a cracked cover are all things a homeowner with basic tools can handle.

Call a professional once the fix requires digging deeper than a couple feet, running new pipe under a patio or walkway, or tying into an existing foundation drain tile system you can't fully trace by hand. Those jobs involve excavation near your foundation, and getting the slope or connection wrong can undo the fix within a season.

You should also call a pro anytime work would put you near buried electrical lines, gas lines, or if concrete needs to be cut to access a drain run. None of that is worth the risk of doing it yourself, and a mistake near a foundation wall tends to cost far more to correct than it would have to hire it out from the start.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How does window well drainage work if there's no visible pipe?

If you dig into the gravel and don't find a pipe, your well is relying on percolation, meaning water is meant to soak directly into the surrounding soil rather than travel through a drain line. This works until the soil compacts or clay content slows absorption, which is why older percolation-only wells are the ones that flood first during heavy rain.

### How deep should the gravel bed be in a window well?

Most properly built window wells have 6 to 12 inches of gravel above the drain opening or soil. The gravel slows water down and filters out fine debris before it reaches the pipe, so a shallow or missing gravel bed is often part of why a drain clogs quickly.

### Does every window well need a connection to the foundation drain system?

Not necessarily. A standalone dry well a few feet away can work just as well if it's sized and dug correctly, since it operates on the same gravity and percolation principles. What matters is that the water has somewhere reliable to go, whether that's the foundation drain tile or its own dedicated pit.
