# Do I Need a Window Well? Here's How to Tell

Stand in your basement and look up at that window. Can you see the ground outside, or does the glass sit below the dirt line with nothing but a wall of soil behind it? If it's the second one, you're probably asking the right question: do I need a window well?

The short answer is yes, if that window sits below grade and it's supposed to serve as an emergency exit or meet code for a bedroom. Here's how to know for sure, what it costs to fix, and what happens if you skip it.

## How to Tell If You Need a Window Well

A window well is the metal, plastic, or concrete enclosure that holds back dirt around a basement window so the window can open fully to the outside. Without one, a below-grade window is just staring into packed soil. It can't function as an exit no matter how big the glass is.

You likely need a window well if any of these are true:

- Your basement window sits at or below ground level and opens against dirt.
- You're finishing a basement room you plan to use as a bedroom.
- A home inspector or appraiser flagged the room as "non-conforming" for sleeping use.
- You're selling a house with a basement bedroom that has no exterior exit.
- Your existing well is rusted through, caving in, or too small for the window it surrounds.

If none of those apply and your basement window sits above grade with open sight lines to the yard, you probably don't need one. ## What Nebraska Code Actually Requires for a Basement Bedroom

The International Residential Code, adopted across most of Nebraska including Lincoln and Lancaster County, is clear on this point. Every sleeping room, including a basement bedroom, needs an emergency escape and rescue opening. If that opening's sill sits below ground level, the code requires a window well sized to let the window open fully and let a person climb out.

That's not a suggestion. It's the rule an inspector checks before signing off on a finished basement, and it's the rule a real estate agent's contractor checks before listing your house as having a "3-bedroom" instead of a "2-bedroom plus office." Lincoln's Building and Safety Department publishes a [typical egress window and well detail](https://www.lincoln.ne.gov/City/Departments/PDS/Building-Safety/Residential-Charts-and-Diagrams/Typical-Egress-Window-and-Well-Detail) that spells out minimum well dimensions, and you'll need a permit (typically $75 to $200 in Lincoln) before work starts.

Minimum specs worth knowing:

- The window opening needs at least 5.7 square feet of clear opening (5.0 square feet on grade-floor windows).
- The sill height can't be more than 44 inches above the finished floor.
- A window well deeper than 44 inches needs a permanent ladder or steps.
- The well itself needs enough horizontal projection, generally 36 inches, for the window to swing or slide open completely.

Read more on the full standard in our post on [IRC egress window code requirements](/blog/irc-egress-window-code-requirements/). If you're buying or selling, it's worth checking with your agent whether a basement bedroom without a compliant exit is being marketed correctly, since that gap tends to surface during inspection either way.

The permit and inspection process itself is fairly quick once the project is scoped correctly. Lincoln-Lancaster County typically reviews and issues a residential egress permit within a few business days, and the final inspection happens after the well and window are installed, before you close up drywall around it. ## Egress Window vs. Basement Window: They're Not the Same Thing

This is where a lot of homeowners get tripped up. A basement window lets light in. An egress window is a specific, code-rated opening built to let a person out during a fire or emergency, with minimum size, sill height, and well requirements attached.

You can have a basement window that looks fine and still fails code as an egress opening, because it's too small, too high off the floor, or missing a well entirely. Swapping the glass for a bigger pane doesn't fix that if the surrounding frame and opening don't meet the dimensions. ## Signs Your Existing Window Well Isn't Doing Its Job

Maybe you already have a window well, but something's off. Watch for these signs that it needs repair or full replacement rather than a patch:

- **Rust holes or bowing walls.** Corrugated steel wells last a couple decades in Nebraska's freeze-thaw cycles before rust eats through. Once you see holes or the wall bulging inward from soil pressure, it's failing.
- **Standing water after rain.** A well without working drainage turns into a small pond, which can leak into your basement and rot the window frame. A properly built well ties into a gravel base and drain line so water moves away from the foundation instead of sitting against it.
- **Cracked or missing cover.** An open or broken cover is a fall and drowning hazard, especially for kids and pets. The Consumer Product Safety Commission lists uncovered wells and similar backyard openings among common in-home drowning risks worth eliminating, a point covered in its [in-home drowning safety guidance](https://www.cpsc.gov/Safety-Education/Safety-Education-Centers/In-Home-Drowning-Safety-Information-Center).
- **Gaps between the well and the foundation.** Settling soil can pull a well away from the house, leaving a channel for water straight into your basement wall.

**Mini example:** In Crete, a couple named Mike and Rosa had a 1980s corrugated steel well that had rusted almost all the way through on one side. After a heavy spring rain, water pooled in the well and seeped past the window seal, soaking their carpet. ## DIY Checks vs. When to Call a Professional

You can do some of this homework yourself before ever picking up the phone. Measure the window opening with a tape measure. Check the sill height off the finished floor. Look at the well for rust, cracks, or standing water. Snap a few photos. If you're comparing quotes from different contractors, ask each one for a written scope, a permit plan, and proof of license and insurance before you sign anything.

What you should not do yourself:

- **Cutting concrete for a new opening.** This involves structural load-bearing walls, and getting it wrong risks foundation damage.
- **Digging a well next to utility lines.** Nebraska requires a call to 811 before any digging, and window wells often sit near buried electrical, gas, or water lines.
- **Working near electrical panels or outlets close to the well.** Water and electricity near an excavation is a serious hazard.
- **Pulling a permit yourself if you're not the general contractor of record.** Lincoln-Lancaster County requires the work to match the permit, and DIY egress cuts are one of the most common reasons a home inspection flags an issue at resale.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I know if my basement window needs a well?

If the window sits at or below ground level with dirt directly behind the glass, it needs a well to open fully and function as an exit. Measure the opening and sill height, then compare them to Nebraska's IRC minimums. If you're unsure, a quick site visit from a licensed installer will settle it.

### Can I skip the window well if I'm not using the room as a bedroom?

Possibly, but only if the room isn't classified as a sleeping room and you're not planning to sell it as one. Home offices, storage, and rec rooms without egress windows are usually fine. The moment a bed goes in, code and resale value both expect a compliant exit.
