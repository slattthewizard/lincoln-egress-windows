# Window Well Drainage Maintenance Checklist

A clogged window well drain can turn a heavy spring storm into two inches of standing water against your basement wall in under an hour. If that water finds a gap in the seal, you're looking at a wet carpet, a ruined mattress, or worse, mold creeping up the drywall. This window well maintenance checklist walks you through exactly what to check, when to check it, and how to keep water moving away from your foundation instead of pooling next to it.

Lincoln's clay-heavy soil and freeze-thaw winters make window wells work harder than most homeowners realize. A well that drains fine in July can be a bathtub by March once the gravel bed compacts and the drain line silts up. Twenty minutes of maintenance a few times a year is cheaper than a wet-basement cleanup, and it's a lot cheaper than replacing a rusted-out well or a warped egress window frame.

Below is the full checklist, broken down by season, plus the warning signs that mean it's time to stop DIYing and call in a professional.

## Why Window Well Drainage Matters More Than You Think

Every window well has one job beyond holding back dirt: get rain and snowmelt away from your window and foundation before it becomes a problem. Most wells rely on a gravel bed over a drain that ties into your foundation drain tile or daylights somewhere on the property. When that path gets blocked, water has nowhere to go but up and in.

For a basement bedroom, this isn't just a nuisance. Nebraska's residential code requires a functioning egress window and well for any basement sleeping room, and an egress well that floods regularly can compromise the window seal over time, which is exactly the kind of defect a home inspector flags during a sale. ## The Core Window Well Drainage Maintenance Checklist

This is the baseline routine every window well in Lincoln and the surrounding counties needs, regardless of season.

- **Clear debris weekly during leaf drop and after storms.** Leaves, grass clippings, and windblown trash sit on top of the gravel and block the cover's drainage gaps.
- **Check the gravel bed for compaction.** Push a stick or screwdriver into the gravel. If it stops within an inch or two, the bed has compacted and needs to be loosened or topped off.
- **Test the drain itself.** Pour a five-gallon bucket of water into the well and time how long it takes to disappear. Anything over 15-20 minutes means a partial clog.
- **Inspect the cover for cracks or gaps.** A cracked polycarbonate cover lets rain in faster than the drain can handle it. See our notes on [cracked-window-well-cover-replacement](/blog/cracked-window-well-cover-replacement/) if yours is chipped or bowed.
- **Look at the well wall for rust, bowing, or separation from the foundation.** Corrugated steel wells rust from the inside out, and a bowing wall is a structural warning sign, not a cosmetic one.
- **Check the window seal and frame for water staining.** Staining at the bottom corners of the frame usually means water has been sitting against the glass longer than it should.
- **Confirm the grading around the well slopes away from the house.** Even a well-maintained drain can't keep up if soil is directing extra runoff straight at the window.

## Seasonal Window Well Maintenance Checklist

Different seasons stress different parts of the system. Here's how to split the checklist across the year.

| Season | Priority Tasks | Time Needed |
|---|---|---|
| Spring | Clear winter debris, test drain flow with a bucket, check gravel compaction after freeze-thaw cycles | 30-45 min per well |
| Summer | Weekly debris checks, inspect cover for UV cracking, trim back plants growing into the well | 10-15 min per visit |
| Fall | Install or clean leaf guards, do a deep gravel refresh before leaves start falling in volume | 30-60 min per well |
| Winter | Clear snow load off covers, check for ice damming at the drain outlet, watch for frost heave along the well wall | 15-20 min per visit |

## Freeze-Thaw Cycles and Lincoln's Clay Soil

Southeast Nebraska sits in a freeze-thaw belt that's rough on anything buried in the ground, including window well drain lines and gravel beds. Water that seeps into small gaps in compacted gravel expands when it freezes, which shifts soil and can crack drain pipe joints over a single hard winter. The [National Weather Service's Omaha/Valley office](https://www.weather.gov/oax/) tracks Lincoln's freeze-thaw pattern closely, and repeated cycles between November and March are typical for our area, which is part of why a fall gravel refresh matters so much before the ground locks up.

Standing water that never fully drains is also a stormwater management issue on a small scale. The EPA's [Soak Up the Rain program](https://www.epa.gov/soakuprain) has useful background on why grading and drainage around a home matter for keeping water away from foundations, the same principle that applies to a window well's gravel bed and drain line.

Clay soil compounds the issue because it drains far slower than sandy or loamy soil, so water that isn't captured by a functioning drain has almost nowhere else to go. If your well was installed without enough gravel depth or a proper drain tie-in, freeze-thaw cycles will expose that shortcut within a year or two. ## When to Stop DIY and Call a Professional

Most of this checklist is safe and simple for any homeowner to handle. But a few situations call for a professional, and pushing forward on your own can turn a moderate repair into an expensive one.

- **The well wall is bowing, cracked, or separating from the foundation.** This is a structural issue, not a maintenance one.
- **Water is getting past the window seal and into the house.** That usually means the frame or glazing has failed, not just the drain.
- **The drain line needs to be dug up or rerouted.** This often involves cutting into landscaping or, in some cases, concrete near the foundation, which isn't a weekend project.
- **You smell sewage or see water backing up from inside the drain.** That can mean a tie-in problem with your foundation drain tile, which needs a licensed contractor.
- **Anything involves electrical conduit or wiring near the well**, such as low-voltage well lighting that's been damaged by water. Don't troubleshoot live wiring near standing water.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How often should I check my window well drainage?

Check debris and cover condition weekly during spring and fall when leaves and storms are most active. Do a full drain test with a bucket of water four times a year, once each season, and after any unusually heavy rain or snowmelt event.

### What's the fastest way to tell if my window well drain is clogged?

Pour a five-gallon bucket of water into the well and watch how fast it drains. If water is still standing after 15-20 minutes, or if it doesn't visibly go down at all, the drain is clogged or the gravel bed has compacted and needs attention.

### Can I clean a window well drain myself, or do I need a professional?

Clearing surface debris and refreshing the top layer of gravel is safe DIY work. If the clog is deeper in the drain line, involves digging near the foundation, or the well wall shows rust or bowing, call a professional so you don't risk foundation or window seal damage.

### Does poor window well drainage affect my home's resale value?

Yes. Home inspectors routinely flag water staining, standing water, or a rusted well during a sale, and a basement bedroom with a non-functioning egress well can raise questions about code compliance. Keeping the well maintained protects both the window and your negotiating position at closing.
