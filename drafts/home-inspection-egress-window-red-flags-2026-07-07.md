## What Home Inspectors Check on a Basement Egress Window

Home inspectors aren't guessing when they flag a basement window. They're checking it against the International Residential Code (IRC), which Nebraska cities and counties adopt as the baseline for what makes a bedroom legal. An inspector generally checks these five things:

- **Net clear opening:** at least 5.7 square feet when the window is fully open (5.0 square feet is allowed for ground-floor windows in some jurisdictions, but basements almost always need 5.7).
- **Minimum clear width:** 20 inches.
- **Minimum clear height:** 24 inches.
- **Sill height:** no more than 44 inches from the finished floor to the bottom of the window opening.
- **Window well size and ladder:** at least 9 square feet of floor area, with a permanent ladder or steps if the well is deeper than 44 inches.

If a window fails any one of these, the inspector will note it, and most buyers' agents will ask for a credit, a repair, or a price reduction before they'll move forward.

## The Egress Window Inspection Red Flags We See Most Often

Not every red flag is created equal. Some are a $200 fix. Others mean tearing into the foundation. Here's what actually shows up on inspection reports around Lincoln.

**Opening too small.** Older basement windows, especially in homes built before the mid-1990s, were often installed as basic hopper or slider windows with no egress requirement in mind. They vent air and let in light, but they don't come close to 5.7 square feet.

**Sill height over 44 inches.** Even when the window itself is large enough, if it sits too high on the wall, a person can't climb out fast in an emergency. Inspectors measure from the finished basement floor, so if you've added flooring or a subfloor since the window went in, that can push you over the limit.

**No window well ladder.** This one surprises a lot of sellers. If the well is deeper than 44 inches, code requires a permanent ladder or built-in steps. A lot of older wells in Lincoln and Waverly have none.

**Window well filled with water, dirt, or debris.** A well that doesn't drain isn't just an eyesore. It traps moisture against the foundation and can make the window impossible to open in an emergency.

**Window painted or nailed shut.** Inspectors test operation. If a window doesn't open without tools, a key, or extra force, that's an automatic red flag even if the opening is the right size.

**Missing window well entirely.** Occasionally we find a basement window sitting flush with grade, with no well at all. Water pools right against the glass every time it rains.

**Bowing or rusted window well wall.** A corrugated steel well that's rusting through or leaning inward from soil pressure won't hold up an inspector's confidence, even if the window itself passes every measurement. This is usually a straightforward [window well installation](/window-well-installation-lincoln/) job rather than a full window replacement.

**No permit on file.** If a previous owner added or replaced the egress window without pulling a permit, some inspectors and appraisers will flag it as unverified work, especially if it's listed as a bedroom.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the most common egress window red flag on a home inspection?

An opening that's too small for code is the most common one we see, usually a hopper or slider window under 5.7 square feet of clear opening. Missing window well ladders and wells full of standing water or debris are close behind. Both are fixable, but the small-opening issue usually costs more since it often means a full cut-in.

### Can a house sale close with a non-compliant egress window?

Yes, it can close, but the room typically can't be counted as a legal bedroom until the window meets code. Many buyers and sellers negotiate a credit at closing or agree the seller will complete the repair before the deal finalizes. Talk to your agent about how it should be disclosed and priced.

### How long does it take to fix an egress window red flag before closing?

A window well replacement or ladder install can usually be done in a day once the permit is pulled, often within a week of the call. A full cut-in with a larger opening and new well typically takes several days of work plus permit and inspection scheduling, so plan for two to three weeks if that's what the report requires.

### Do all basement windows need to meet egress code?

No. Only basement rooms used or marketed as bedrooms are required to have a code-compliant egress window and well. A basement window in a laundry room, mechanical space, or unfinished storage area doesn't need to meet the same size and sill height rules, though it still needs to open and drain properly.
