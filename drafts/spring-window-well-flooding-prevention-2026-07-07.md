## Why Spring Is Prime Time for Window Well Flooding

Winter sets the trap and spring springs it. Frozen ground can't absorb water, so when temperatures swing above freezing and a rainstorm lands on top of half-melted snow, that water has nowhere to go but sideways, toward the lowest point around your foundation. A window well, being a hole dug next to that foundation, is often exactly that low point.

Add in the fact that gutters have spent all winter collecting fall leaves nobody cleaned out, and grading that shifted slightly from frost heave, and you've got a setup where a single strong storm overwhelms a system that worked fine last July. Lincoln's own freeze-thaw cycles make this worse over time by settling soil and cracking well seams, something we cover in more depth in our [Nebraska freeze-thaw window well damage](/blog/nebraska-freeze-thaw-window-well-damage/) article.

The [National Weather Service office covering southeast Nebraska](https://www.weather.gov/oax/) tracks this pattern every year: the region's heaviest rain events cluster in spring, right as frozen soil is thawing and least able to drain. That's not a coincidence homeowners can ignore. It's the exact reason prevention work needs to happen in February and March, not April.

## Prevent Spring Window Well Flooding by Fixing Your Grading First

Grading is the single biggest factor in whether a window well floods, and it's also the one people check last. If the soil around your foundation slopes toward the house instead of away from it, every storm sends water straight at your window well no matter how good the drain underneath is.

Walk your foundation after the ground thaws and look at the slope within 6 to 10 feet of the house. It should drop away from the foundation, roughly a 6-inch drop over that distance is the general rule of thumb. If you see a low spot funneling toward a window well, or mulch and soil that has settled flat or reversed over the years, that's your flood risk right there.

Small grading corrections, adding and packing soil to restore the slope, are a weekend DIY project for a lot of homeowners. Larger regrading jobs, especially ones involving a full section of yard or tying into a drainage system, run about $300 to $900 done professionally. [Nebraska Extension's water resources program](https://water.unl.edu/) has solid background on managing runoff around a home if you want to understand the grading and drainage side before deciding whether to DIY it.

Don't stop at the well itself. Landscaping beds, downspout splash blocks, and even a newly poured patio can all redirect water toward a well without anyone intending it. Trace where water actually goes during the next light rain and you'll usually spot the problem in ten minutes.

## Clean Gutters, Downspouts, and Drains Before the First Storm

A clogged gutter doesn't just overflow onto your lawn. It dumps water in a concentrated stream right next to your foundation, and if a window well happens to sit under or near that downspout, you've basically built a funnel straight into your basement.

Clear gutters of fall leaves and winter debris before spring storms start, and check that every downspout extends at least 4 to 6 feet from the foundation. A lot of Lincoln homes still have downspouts that dump water two feet from the house, which was fine decades ago when the grading was better but isn't doing you any favors now. Downspout extensions cost as little as $20 to $40 per spout at a hardware store, making this one of the cheapest fixes on this entire list.

While you're up there, check the window well itself for the same kind of debris. Leaves and dirt that collected over winter will choke a well's drain the moment real rain hits it. Our [window well leaf and debris cleanup](/blog/window-well-leaf-debris-cleanup/) guide walks through how to clear it out safely without damaging the well liner or the window seal.

Then test the actual drain. Pour a few gallons of water into the well on a dry day and watch how fast it disappears. If it drains within 20 to 30 minutes, you're in decent shape. If it sits there or drains at a crawl, the gravel bed underneath is likely silted up or the drain line has failed, and that's worth addressing before storm season, not after. ## Covers and Seals: Cheap Insurance Against Spring Window Well Flooding

If your window well doesn't have a cover, that's the fastest fix on this list to close the gap on spring window well flooding. An open well collects every drop of rain and every bit of snowmelt directly, on top of whatever runoff is already reaching it from bad grading or gutters.

A properly fitted, secured cover keeps the bulk of precipitation out entirely and stops leaves and debris from clogging the gravel bed underneath. Clear polycarbonate covers are popular because they still let daylight into the basement, while solid covers offer a bit more protection in heavy weather. Covers and ladders together typically run $150 to $600 depending on well size and material, and installation usually takes under an hour.

Check any existing cover for cracks, warping, or a loose fit before storm season starts. A cracked cover can actually make things worse by channeling water to one spot instead of spreading it, and a loose-fitting one still lets in more rain than most people expect. If yours is more than a few years old and hasn't held up to Nebraska winters well, replacing it now costs a fraction of what a flooded basement does.

## When to Call a Professional

Most of what's in this guide, clearing debris, testing drainage, extending a downspout, is safe and reasonable to handle yourself. A few things aren't.

If your test shows the well barely drains at all, the problem is likely underground, either a collapsed drain line or a gravel bed that was never properly connected to your sump system in the first place. That's excavation work, and digging into it without knowing what's down there can do more damage than the flooding itself.

Any work that involves cutting concrete for a new drain tie-in, or that gets close to electrical panels and outlets near a window well, should go to a licensed contractor. Cracking or bowing in the well wall is a structural issue rather than a drainage one, and it's worth a professional look before it gets worse under repeated water pressure. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### When should I start prevention work to beat spring flooding?

Late winter, ideally February or early March in Lincoln, before the ground fully thaws and the first big storms arrive. Grading and drainage work is easier and cleaner on dry, cool days than it is once spring rain has already started falling.

### How do I know if my window well drainage is already failing?

Pour several gallons of water into the well on a dry day and time how long it takes to disappear. Anything under 30 minutes is healthy. If water sits for hours or doesn't drain at all, the gravel bed or drain line likely needs attention before storm season.

### Can I prevent flooding with just a cover, or do I need drainage work too?

A cover alone helps a lot if your well already drains reasonably well and your main problem is rain and debris collecting directly. If water pools even without rain falling into the well, grading or the drain underneath is the real issue, and a cover won't fix that on its own.

### Is prevention actually cheaper than dealing with a flood when it happens?

In nearly every case we see. A downspout extension or regrading job runs a few hundred dollars done proactively. The same property after a flood often faces cleanup, drywall repair, and sometimes a full window or well replacement, easily several times the cost of the prevention work that would have stopped it.
