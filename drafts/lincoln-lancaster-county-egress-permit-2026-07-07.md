## Do You Need an Egress Window Permit in Lincoln?

Short answer: almost always, yes. Any time you're cutting a new opening in a foundation wall, enlarging an existing window opening, or adding a below-grade bedroom, that work triggers a permit under the residential building code Lincoln has adopted. Straight glass swaps in an existing frame, with no change to the opening size, sometimes don't need one, but it's worth a five-minute call to confirm before you assume you're in the clear.

The trigger isn't really "did I buy a window." It's "did I change the structure or create a sleeping room that needs a legal exit." A basement bedroom without a code-compliant egress window isn't a legal bedroom no matter how nice the paint job is. Appraisers and home inspectors know this, and so do buyers' agents.

## What Lincoln Building & Safety Requires for the Permit

Lincoln Building & Safety wants a few basic things before they'll issue an egress window permit: a site plan or sketch showing where the opening goes, the rough opening dimensions, and confirmation the window meets the minimum clear opening, sill height, and well dimensions spelled out in the residential code. Structural work, like cutting through a load-bearing foundation wall, may need an engineer's letter depending on the scope.

Once the permit is issued, expect at least one inspection. Most jurisdictions in this area require a rough-in inspection after the opening is cut but before the well and finish work go in, so the inspector can see the header, the sill, and the clear opening before anything gets covered up. A final inspection often follows once the well, drainage, and any ladder or steps are in place.

This is one reason DIY egress cuts go sideways. If you pour a well or finish the drywall before the rough-in inspection, you may have to open it back up. We've written about the full process in our [egress window cut-in process](/blog/egress-window-cut-in-process/) post if you want the step-by-step version.

For the official code language, the City of Lincoln's building permit and inspection requirements are published through [Lincoln Building & Safety](https://lincoln.ne.gov/city/build/) and reference the International Residential Code sections on emergency escape and rescue openings.

Either the homeowner or the licensed contractor doing the work can pull the permit, but there's a catch worth knowing. If you pull it yourself and something doesn't meet code, the fix and the delay are on you. When a licensed crew pulls the permit as part of the job, they're the ones accountable for getting the inspection right the first time, which is usually worth more than the fee itself.

## What Happens If You Skip the Permit

Some homeowners figure a permit is optional if the work is "just a window." It isn't, and the consequences show up at the worst possible times. Unpermitted egress work can surface during a home sale, a refinance appraisal, or an insurance claim after storm damage, and each of those situations comes with its own headache.

If Building & Safety catches unpermitted structural work in progress, they can issue a stop-work order until you pull a permit and get it inspected, sometimes with a penalty fee on top of the normal cost. If it's discovered after the fact, during a sale for example, you may need to open up finished work to prove it meets code, which is far more expensive than getting the permit up front.

There's also the resale angle. A basement bedroom that isn't legally a bedroom hurts your listing price and can stall a closing while the seller scrambles to fix it. Lenders and appraisers increasingly check for this. It's cheaper and less stressful to handle the permit before the drywall goes up than to defend the work after the fact.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do I need a permit for a basic egress window replacement in Lincoln?

If you're swapping a window into the same opening with no change in size, you often don't need a new permit. If you're enlarging the opening, adding a well, or creating a new opening, you do. Call Lincoln Building & Safety or your contractor to confirm before work starts.

### How much does an egress window permit cost in Lincoln or Lancaster County?

Expect $75 to $200 for the permit itself in most cases. That's separate from the cost of the window, the cut-in labor, and the window well, which for a full new installation commonly runs $3,500 to $7,000 or more depending on scope.

### How long does the permit and inspection process take?

For a routine residential job, figure one to three weeks from permit application to final sign-off, including the rough-in and final inspections. Scheduling inspections usually takes one to three business days once you request them.

### What happens if I sell my house with an unpermitted egress window?

It can slow down or derail your closing. Home inspectors and appraisers look for code-compliant egress in any room marketed as a bedroom, and an unpermitted or non-compliant window may require retroactive permitting and repair work before the sale can go through.
