## Why Nebraska Basements Are the Default Tornado Shelter

Lancaster, Seward, Saline, and Gage counties sit squarely in tornado alley's eastern edge, and most local building stock reflects that. Basements are common, and for good reason: being below grade puts mass and soil between you and flying debris, which is the main thing that hurts people in a tornado.

The National Weather Service is blunt about the hierarchy. A basement or storm shelter beats an interior room on the lowest floor, which beats a mobile home or car every time. If your house has a basement, that's where you go. You can read the full guidance on the [NWS tornado safety page](https://www.weather.gov/safety/tornado).

None of that changes because a basement has a window. The window well itself isn't a weak point in a tornado. It's a small, sunken area outside the foundation wall that lets light and air into a below-grade room. What matters is what's connected to that well: is there a way out of the basement if the stairs are blocked by debris, and is the window big enough for a person to actually use it.

## Tornado Shelter Basement Window Well Safety: What Egress Actually Changes

Here's the part people miss. An egress window's main safety job during a tornado isn't keeping wind out. It's giving you a second way out if the primary exit gets buried.

Nebraska tornadoes are usually short-lived and narrow compared to the monsters you see in national news footage, but they do drop trees on houses, collapse porches onto stairwells, and jam doors with debris. If your basement stairway happens to be on the side of the house that takes the hit, a code-sized egress window with a functioning well is your way out without waiting for someone to dig you free.

That's a different job than a purpose-built storm shelter or safe room, and it's worth being honest about the difference. An egress window is not rated to resist flying debris the way a FEMA-spec safe room is. What it does is turn a basement that's already your safest room into one you can also escape from, which matters most after the tornado passes, when you're checking for gas leaks, fire, or people trapped upstairs.

## What Nebraska Code Requires for Basement Bedrooms (and Why It Matters in a Storm)

Lincoln follows the International Residential Code (IRC) for egress, which sets specific minimums for any room used as a sleeping space below grade:

- A clear openable area of at least 5.7 square feet (5.0 square feet if the opening is at grade level)
- Minimum opening height of 24 inches and minimum width of 20 inches
- Sill height no more than 44 inches above the floor
- A window well if the window sits below grade, with enough space for the window to open fully
- A permanent ladder or steps if the well is deeper than 44 inches

These numbers aren't arbitrary. They're sized so an adult in bulky clothing, or a firefighter in gear, can actually get a person out. That's the same standard that makes a basement usable as a fallback exit during severe weather. A window that technically lets light in but is too small to climb through doesn't help anyone during or after a tornado.

## Window Well Safety Checklist Before Storm Season

A window well that's neglected doesn't just leak or fill with leaves. It can turn a good escape route into a mud pit you can't climb out of. Run through this before Nebraska's severe weather season ramps up in earnest:

1. **Clear debris.** Leaves, sticks, and dirt buildup block drainage and give the well less usable depth.
2. **Check the drain.** If your well has a drain tied into the footing drain system, make sure it isn't clogged. Standing water freezes and expands against the foundation in colder months too.
3. **Test the cover.** Covers should lift or unlatch easily from inside. A cover that's rusted shut or overgrown with landscaping defeats the purpose.
4. **Confirm the ladder, if the well is deep.** Any well deeper than 44 inches needs a fixed ladder or steps. Loose or rusted rungs need replacing before you need them.
5. **Open the window itself.** Paint, swollen wood frames, and old hardware can freeze a window shut. Test it at least once a season, not for the first time during a warning.

## When a Window Well Becomes a DIY Project You Should Skip

Plenty of window well maintenance is reasonable to handle yourself: clearing leaves, tightening a loose cover latch, touching up caulk around the frame. Cutting a new opening into your foundation is not.

Enlarging or adding an egress window means cutting concrete or block, which can affect the structural integrity of the foundation wall if it's not done correctly. It also usually means digging below grade next to your foundation, coordinating drainage so water doesn't route toward the house instead of away from it, and pulling a Lincoln building permit ($75-$200) so the work is inspected and legal.

Stop and call a professional if the project involves:

- Cutting concrete or block for a new or larger opening
- Any excavation deeper than a couple of feet next to the foundation
- Electrical lines or conduit running near the well area
- A foundation wall that's already showing cracks or bowing

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does a window well make a basement less safe during a tornado?

No. The well and window themselves don't weaken the room's protection from wind and debris. What matters is whether the window is large enough to serve as a backup exit and whether the well drains properly so it doesn't fill with water or debris that would block that exit.

### Is a basement always the safest place during a tornado warning?

For most Lincoln-area homes, yes, a basement beats any above-ground room. The exception is a basement directly under a garage or with large windows facing the likely wind direction; in those cases, get to the most interior, lowest area you can, away from windows. The [NWS tornado safety page](https://www.weather.gov/safety/tornado) has room-by-room guidance if you're unsure about your layout.

### Do I need an egress window if I just use my basement as a rec room, not a bedroom?

Code only requires egress windows for basement bedrooms, but many homeowners add one anyway for the added exit option and because it makes future finishing or a bedroom conversion easier. If you're considering finishing the space, our [basement finishing with egress in Lincoln](/blog/basement-finishing-egress-lincoln/) post covers how to plan it in from the start.

### What should I keep in my basement for tornado shelter, beyond the egress window?

The Ready.gov emergency supply guidance recommends flashlights, a battery or hand-crank radio, sturdy shoes, and a basic first aid kit stored where you actually shelter, not in a closet upstairs. See [ready.gov's tornado preparedness page](https://www.ready.gov/tornadoes) for the full checklist. Pair that with a window well you've already tested this season, and your basement is about as ready as it can be.
