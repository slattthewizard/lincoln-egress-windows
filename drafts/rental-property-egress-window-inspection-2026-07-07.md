## What Makes a Window "Egress" Under Nebraska and Lincoln Rental Codes

An egress window isn't just any window in a basement bedroom. It has to meet specific size and access rules adopted from the International Residential Code (IRC), which Nebraska and Lancaster County use as the baseline for building safety.

The general numbers most Lincoln inspectors check:

- Minimum clear opening of 5.7 square feet (5.0 square feet is allowed for ground-floor windows in some cases)
- Minimum clear opening height of 24 inches
- Minimum clear opening width of 20 inches
- Sill height no more than 44 inches above the finished floor
- A window well with at least 9 square feet of horizontal area and 36 inches of horizontal projection, if the window sits below grade
- A permanent ladder or steps if the window well is deeper than 44 inches

A window that's technically "there" but doesn't hit those numbers, or is blocked by a security bar without a quick-release mechanism, will not pass. That's true whether the property is owner-occupied or a rental, but rentals get scrutinized harder because a tenant didn't choose the risk. They're just living with whatever the landlord installed or inherited.

Lincoln and Lancaster County require a building permit for most egress window installations and replacements that change the opening size, cut new concrete, or add a window well. Simple like-for-like sash replacements in an opening that already meets code typically don't need one, but anything involving a new cut-in almost always does.

Permit costs in Lincoln generally run $75 to $200, and the inspection that follows is where a rental property egress window inspection actually happens on paper. An inspector measures the clear opening, checks the well dimensions, and confirms the sill height and any ladder requirements. If you skip the permit and do work anyway, that gap shows up the moment anyone official looks at the property, and it's far more expensive to fix retroactively than to do right the first time.

## What a Rental Property Egress Window Inspection Checks, Item by Item

Whether the inspection is done by a city official, a home inspector during a sale, or a contractor doing a pre-listing walkthrough, the checklist looks about the same:

1. **Opening size.** Measured with the window fully open, not the frame size on the label.
2. **Operability.** The window has to open without keys, tools, or special knowledge. Painted-shut or stuck sashes fail immediately.
3. **Sill height.** Too high above the floor and a child or an older tenant can't reach it in an emergency.
4. **Window well dimensions and drainage.** A well that's too small, too shallow, or holds standing water is a fail.
5. **Ladder or steps.** Required once the well is deeper than 44 inches, and it has to stay clear of clutter or debris.
6. **Glazing.** Tempered glass is required in most egress locations near grade, especially close to doors or low sill heights.
7. **Security bars or grilles.** Allowed only if they release from the inside without a key or tool.

Missing even one of these can flag the whole bedroom as non-compliant, which is why a full inspection, not just a glance at the window, is worth doing before you list a rental or renew a lease that depends on that bedroom's rent.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do all basement bedrooms in Lincoln rentals legally require an egress window?

Yes. If a basement room is used or advertised as a bedroom, it needs a code-compliant egress window regardless of whether the property is owner-occupied or a rental. Without it, the room isn't a legal bedroom and can't be counted as one for rent, appraisal, or occupancy purposes.

### How often should a landlord check egress windows in a rental?

A quick check at every tenant turnover and again during your annual maintenance walkthrough is enough for most properties. Look for stuck sashes, cracked wells, standing water, and any security bars that don't release freely from the inside.

### Can a tenant refuse to pay rent if the egress window fails inspection?

Tenants may have grounds to negotiate rent or terminate a lease if a bedroom loses its legal status, since you can't charge bedroom-level rent for a non-compliant space. It's far cheaper to fix the window than to fight that dispute later.

### What happens if I skip the permit for an egress window repair?

Unpermitted work can be flagged during a future inspection, sale, or refinance, and you may be required to open the wall or well back up for a proper inspection before it's approved retroactively. Pulling the $75-$200 permit up front avoids that cost and delay entirely.
