## Why Hiring the Right Egress Window Contractor in Lincoln Matters

An egress window isn't a cosmetic upgrade. Under the code Lincoln enforces, any basement bedroom needs an operable emergency escape opening of at least 5.7 square feet, with a minimum 20-inch width and 24-inch height when open, and a sill no higher than 44 inches off the floor. Get any of those numbers wrong and the window won't pass inspection, no matter how nice it looks.

That's the part homeowners underestimate when they're comparing bids. A contractor who skips the permit or eyeballs the opening size might save you a few hundred dollars up front. But if you go to sell the house and the buyer's inspector flags a basement bedroom with no legal egress, you're looking at a price renegotiation or a rushed fix under deadline pressure, which almost always costs more than doing it right the first time.

## The Checklist: How to Hire an Egress Window Contractor in Lincoln Step by Step

Use this as a working list while you're collecting bids. Don't skip steps just because a contractor seems friendly on the phone.

1. **Confirm they're licensed and insured for Nebraska work.** Ask for proof, not just a verbal "yep." A contractor who hesitates to send documentation is telling you something.
2. **Ask who pulls the permit.** In Lincoln, egress window cut-ins and window well excavation typically require a permit filed through the city's [Building and Safety permit applications](https://www.lincoln.ne.gov/City/Departments/PDS/Building-Safety/Permit-Applications) process. If a contractor says permits aren't necessary for this kind of job, that's a wrong answer, not a shortcut.
3. **Get the opening dimensions in writing.** The quote should state the finished opening size, not just "egress window installed." Nebraska's minimum is 5.7 square feet with 20-inch minimum width and 24-inch minimum height.
4. **Ask how they handle the foundation cut.** Concrete cutting near a basement wall is structural work. You want a contractor who explains how they support the opening and manage the excavation, not one who glosses over it.
5. **Ask about drainage.** A window well without a proper drain, or one tied incorrectly into an existing system, is the number one cause of flooded basements after installation. Ask specifically what drainage solution is included.
6. **Compare warranty terms, not just price.** A cheap install with no warranty on the seal or the well is a bet you're making with your own basement.
7. **Read the fine print on what's excluded.** Interior trim, drywall patching, and window well covers are sometimes billed separately. Know that before you compare two numbers side by side.

That seven-point list is the core of how to hire an egress window contractor in Lincoln without getting a surprise later. If a bid fails more than one of these checks, treat it as a warning sign rather than a bargain.

## Questions to Ask Before You Sign Anything

A short phone call before you sign a contract can save you weeks of headaches. Ask these directly:

- "Will you file the permit, or is that on me?"
- "What's the finished opening size, in square feet?"
- "What happens to the excavated dirt and the old window well?"
- "What's your timeline from signed contract to final inspection?"
- "What's covered under warranty, and for how long?"
- "Who do I call if the well floods after a heavy rain?"

## Red Flags That Mean Walk Away

Some warning signs are easy to miss when you're focused on price. These aren't unique to egress windows either. The [Federal Trade Commission's guide to hiring a contractor](https://www.consumer.ftc.gov/articles/0242-hiring-contractor) covers a lot of the same ground for any home improvement project, and it's worth a quick read before you sign anything. For egress windows specifically, watch for these:

- **Cash-only, no written contract.** A verbal agreement gives you no protection if the work fails inspection or the well floods.
- **"No permit needed."** This is almost never true for a structural cut-in in Lincoln, and skipping it can come back to bite you at resale.
- **Vague opening dimensions.** If the quote just says "egress window" with no square footage listed, you can't verify it meets code.
- **Pressure to sign same-day.** Legitimate contractors expect homeowners to compare bids. Urgency tactics are a sales technique, not a sign of good service.
- **No mention of drainage.** Skipping this conversation entirely is one of the most common ways cheap installs turn into expensive flooding problems later.

## When to Call a Professional (Instead of DIYing It)

Some homeowners want to handle part of the project themselves, and that's fine for tasks like clearing debris from an existing well or repainting trim. But concrete cutting into a foundation wall is structural work, and it's not a weekend project. The same goes for excavation deep enough to expose electrical conduit or gas lines near the foundation.

If your project involves any of the following, stop and call a licensed contractor: cutting a new opening where none exists, working near buried utility lines, or dealing with a foundation wall that already shows cracking or bowing.
