# Tempered Glass Egress Window vs. Laminated: Which One Does Your Basement Need?

Your basement bedroom window just cracked, or you're about to cut a new opening for one, and now someone's asking whether you need tempered glass or laminated glass. It's not a small question. Pick wrong and you could fail a home inspection, fail a city permit inspection, or pay twice because the wrong glass has to come back out.

Here's the short version: most egress windows in Lincoln use tempered glass because it's the standard safety glazing that meets code and it's the cheaper of the two. Laminated glass is an upgrade some homeowners choose for extra storm or security protection, and it costs more. Both satisfy the "tempered glass egress window" requirement in a general sense, since both count as safety glazing, but they behave very differently once something hits them.

## What's the Difference Between Tempered and Laminated Glass?

Tempered glass is heat-treated so it's about four times stronger than regular annealed glass. When it finally breaks, it shatters into small, dull-edged pebbles instead of long dangerous shards. That's why it's required in most window and door locations near the floor, including egress windows.

Laminated glass is two layers of glass bonded around a clear plastic interlayer, similar to a windshield. It can crack under impact, but the plastic layer holds the pieces together instead of letting them fall out. That makes it much harder to punch through, even after it's broken.

Both types qualify as "safety glazing" under residential building code, which is the category egress windows fall into. The difference shows up in how each one performs during a storm, a break-in attempt, or a kid running into the glass with a bike.

## Why Lincoln Code Requires Safety Glass Near Basement Bedrooms

Egress windows sit low, close to grade, and often right next to a window well with a metal or composite well liner. That's a spot where regular glass is a real hazard if it breaks, whether from a fall, a lawn mower rock, or a hard freeze-thaw cycle cracking the well wall against the frame.

The International Residential Code (IRC), which Lincoln and Lancaster County use as the basis for local permitting, requires safety glazing in "hazardous locations," and window openings near grade in a basement sleeping room fall into that category. You can read the underlying federal safety glazing standard directly from the Consumer Product Safety Commission at [cpsc.gov](https://www.cpsc.gov/Regulations-Laws--Standards/Statutes/Architectural-Glazing), which is the standard most tempered and laminated glass products are certified against.

A tempered glass egress window meets that requirement in its most common, lowest-cost form. Laminated glass meets it too, and adds a layer of impact resistance tempered glass doesn't have. Neither one is "wrong" from a code standpoint. The question is really about what extra protection you want to pay for.

If your home is in Lincoln proper, the [city's building permit process](https://www.lincoln.ne.gov/City/Departments/Building-and-Safety) will check glazing type along with the size and sill height of the opening. ## Tempered Glass Egress Windows: Cost, Pros, and Cons

Tempered glass is the default for a reason. It's widely stocked, it's less expensive to manufacture, and every reputable window supplier in the region carries egress units with it standard. For most basement bedroom projects, it's the right call.

**Pros:**
- Lower material cost, which keeps the whole job closer to the bottom of the price range
- Breaks into small, blunt pieces instead of sharp shards
- Meets IRC safety glazing requirements for hazardous locations
- Available in nearly every casement, slider, and inswing egress style we install

**Cons:**
- Once it breaks, it's gone. There's no plastic layer holding pieces in the frame, so you get an open hole until it's replaced
- No extra resistance to forced entry or high wind debris
- Can spontaneously break years later from a manufacturing flaw called nickel sulfide inclusion, though this is rare

## Laminated Glass: Extra Storm and Break-In Protection

Laminated glass costs more, usually adding a few hundred dollars per window over a standard tempered unit, but some homeowners want that upgrade for specific reasons. Southeast Nebraska sees its share of severe spring storms, and a basement is often the safest room in the house during a tornado warning.

Because laminated glass holds together after impact, it's a common choice for homeowners building or upgrading a basement space into a storm shelter or safe room. It also resists forced entry better than tempered glass, since an intruder has to cut or tear through the plastic interlayer even after cracking the outer pane, not just knock a hole through.

## Choosing the Right Glass for Your Lincoln Home

For most basement bedroom conversions, tempered glass is the practical choice. It's code-compliant, it's the standard everyone stocks, and it keeps your project inside the typical $5,500-$9,500 range for a full Lincoln installation. If you're finishing a basement purely to meet code and add a legal bedroom, there's rarely a reason to spend more.

Laminated glass makes sense when you have a specific reason to want it: a storm safe room, a security concern, or a busy street where flying debris is a real risk. It's an upgrade, not a requirement, and it's one of the more affordable ways to add real protection to a basement space.

Either way, the glass is only half the equation. The opening has to meet minimum size and sill height rules, and the well itself needs proper drainage so water isn't sitting against your foundation and your window frame all spring. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is a tempered glass egress window required by code, or can I use regular glass?

Regular annealed glass isn't allowed in egress window openings near grade. The IRC classifies these as hazardous locations, which requires safety glazing, either tempered or laminated. Regular glass will fail both a building permit inspection and most home inspections.

### Does laminated glass cost a lot more than tempered glass for an egress window?

Not dramatically. Most homeowners see laminated glass add roughly $250-$500 per window compared to standard tempered glazing. On a full install running $5,500-$9,500, that's a small percentage increase for meaningfully better impact resistance.

### Can I tell whether my existing basement window has tempered or laminated glass?

Tempered glass usually has a small etched logo or stamp in one corner, often from the manufacturer or a certification body. Laminated glass is visibly thicker with a plastic layer sandwiched in the middle, though the easiest way to know for sure is to have a contractor check it during an inspection.

### Should I replace the glass myself or hire a contractor?

Swapping glass alone, without touching the frame or the opening, is sometimes doable for a handy homeowner with the right glazing tools. But if the opening needs resizing, the frame is damaged, or the window well needs work, that involves cutting concrete and structural considerations that call for a licensed professional. When in doubt, get a free estimate before you start cutting.
