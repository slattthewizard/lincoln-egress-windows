## Egress Window Minimum Size Requirements: The Three Numbers That Matter

Every Nebraska jurisdiction, including Lincoln-Lancaster County, enforces egress sizing through the IRC. Three measurements decide whether a basement window is legal.

**Net clear opening: 5.7 square feet minimum.** This is the actual usable opening once the window is fully open, not the size of the frame or the rough opening in your wall. Grade-floor openings (windows at or near ground level) get a slightly lower bar at 5.0 square feet.

**Minimum height: 24 inches.** The opening has to be at least 24 inches tall when the window is fully open, measured on the clear opening, not the glass.

**Minimum width: 20 inches.** Same idea, measured horizontally. A window can pass the height minimum and still fail on width, or the other way around. Both have to check out at the same time.

There's a fourth number that trips people up because it's not about the window itself: sill height. The bottom of the clear opening can't be more than 44 inches above the finished floor. A code-size window mounted too high still fails.

## How to Measure Your Window's Net Clear Opening

Net clear opening is the number that actually matters, and it's the one people measure wrong most often.

Open the window all the way. For a casement window that cranks outward, measure the actual open gap, width and height, not the frame dimensions on the box it came in. For a slider or single-hung window, only half the frame typically opens, so the net clear opening is roughly half the window's total size, sometimes less once you subtract the frame and sash thickness.

Multiply width in inches by height in inches, then divide by 144 to get square feet. A 30-inch by 24-inch opening comes out to 5.0 square feet, which fails the standard 5.7 requirement even though both individual dimensions look fine on paper.

This is exactly why a window that "looks big enough" can still fail inspection. The math has to work, not just the eyeball test. If you're not confident in your measurement, a contractor can check it in about ten minutes, often as part of a free estimate.

## Common Window Sizes That Meet (and Miss) Nebraska's Minimum Size Requirements

Manufacturers publish net clear opening specs for every model, and they vary by brand even at the same nominal size. Use this table as a starting point, then confirm against the actual spec sheet for the unit you're considering.

| Nominal Window Size | Approx. Net Clear Opening | Meets 5.7 Sq Ft Minimum? |
|---|---|---|
| 24" x 24" slider | ~3.3 sq ft | No |
| 30" x 24" casement | ~5.0 sq ft | No |
| 32" x 24" casement | ~5.3 sq ft | No, close |
| 36" x 24" casement | ~6.0 sq ft | Yes |
| 38" x 24" casement | ~6.3 sq ft | Yes |
| 32" x 30" casement | ~6.7 sq ft | Yes |
| 24" x 36" casement (vertical) | ~6.0 sq ft | Yes |

A few patterns show up here. Sliders and single-hung windows almost never pass egress because only part of the sash opens, which is why nearly every code-compliant basement window in Lincoln is a casement that swings open completely. Second, 24 inches tall is the bare floor for height, so most compliant windows are wider than 32 inches to make up the area on the width side instead of relying on a taller, deeper well.

If your existing window is a slider or single-hung under 36 inches wide, it's very likely too small no matter how it looks from the outside.

## Sill Height, Window Wells, and Other Size Rules That Trip People Up

Getting the window itself right is only part of the job. A few related measurements matter just as much.

**Sill height (44 inches max).** Even a perfectly sized window fails if it's mounted too high on the wall for someone to climb out. This comes up a lot in older homes where the window was placed for light, not exit.

**Window well size (9 square feet minimum).** If your window sits below grade, the well around it needs at least 9 square feet of horizontal area, with no dimension less than 36 inches. A well that's too narrow blocks the window from opening fully even if the window itself is code-size.

**Ladder requirement (wells deeper than 44 inches).** Deep wells need a permanently attached ladder or steps so someone can actually climb out, not just have room to stand.

Skipping any of these can fail an otherwise correctly sized window. This is also where DIY work gets risky. Cutting a wider opening, deepening a well near a foundation wall, or working around buried electrical lines near the well should go to a licensed contractor, not a weekend project. Once you know your window fails, the fix depends on how far off it is.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the minimum egress window size in Nebraska?

Nebraska follows the IRC, which requires a net clear opening of at least 5.7 square feet (5.0 for grade-floor windows), a minimum height of 24 inches, and a minimum width of 20 inches. The sill can't sit more than 44 inches above the floor.

### Does a window that's 20 inches wide and 24 inches tall automatically pass?

Not necessarily. Those are the minimum height and width, but the window also has to meet the 5.7 square foot net clear opening area, and a 20-by-24 window (3.3 square feet) usually falls short. All three numbers have to work together, and the operable sash type matters as much as the frame size.

### Can I keep my current window if it's close to the minimum?

If your window fails, "close" doesn't count for inspection purposes. Get the exact net clear opening measured, since sliders and single-hung windows often look adequate on the frame but only open partway. A casement swap in the same opening is often cheaper than most homeowners expect.

### What size does my window well need to be for a code-compliant window?

The well needs at least 9 square feet of horizontal area, with no dimension smaller than 36 inches, so the window can open fully. Wells deeper than 44 inches also need a permanently attached ladder or steps.
