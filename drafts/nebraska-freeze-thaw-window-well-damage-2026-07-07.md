## What Happens During a Nebraska Freeze-Thaw Cycle

Nebraska's weather doesn't do winter gently. A day in the 40s can drop into the single digits by nightfall, and that swing can happen a dozen times or more between November and March. The [National Weather Service office](https://www.weather.gov/oax/) that covers Lincoln tracks these rapid temperature swings every winter, and they're a normal part of our climate, not a rare event. Each swing forces moisture in the soil, in cracks, and in construction joints to freeze and expand by roughly 9 percent, then thaw and contract again.

That expansion is called frost heave, and it's strong enough to lift sidewalks, crack foundations, and buckle window well walls. Frost depth in the Lincoln area typically reaches down 30 to 40 inches in a cold winter, according to regional data from the University of Nebraska-Lincoln's [Institute of Agriculture and Natural Resources](https://extension.unl.edu/), which studies soil freezing and its effects on structures and drainage. Window wells sit right in that active freeze zone, which is exactly why they take so much abuse.

A single freeze-thaw cycle won't crack a steel well or split a window frame. It's the repetition that does it. Year after year, the same joints and welds get stressed a little more until something finally gives.

## Warning Signs of Freeze-Thaw Window Well Damage

You don't need to be a contractor to spot early freeze-thaw window well damage. Walk around your foundation on a mild day and look for these signs:

- A well wall that leans, bulges, or looks bowed compared to the foundation
- Cracks or splits in a corrugated metal or polycarbonate cover
- A visible gap opening up between the well and the foundation wall
- Rust streaks or flaking paint at the seams of a metal well
- Standing water, mud, or ice buildup at the bottom of the well
- A basement window that sticks, won't seal, or lets in a draft

Any one of these on its own might not be urgent. Two or three together usually mean the freeze-thaw cycle has been working on that well for a while.

## How Frost Heave Cracks Covers, Frames, and Drains

Freeze-thaw damage doesn't stop at the well wall. It works on every part of the system.

**Covers.** Cheaper plastic and older polycarbonate covers get brittle in cold weather. When ice forms on top and then thaws, the repeated flexing cracks the plastic at the mounting points first. Once a cover is cracked, more water gets into the well, which feeds the next freeze cycle. Our guide on [cracked window well cover replacement](/blog/cracked-window-well-cover-replacement/) covers what to look for and when a $150 to $600 replacement makes more sense than a patch job.

**Window frames.** Ice that forms in the gap between an egress window frame and the rough opening can pry the frame away from the foundation a fraction of an inch at a time. Over several winters that gap widens, seals fail, and water starts tracking into the basement around the frame instead of just the well.

**Drains.** Window well drains rely on gravel and a clear line to daylight or a sump. When ice forms in that gravel bed or freezes solid in the drain line, meltwater has nowhere to go. It backs up against the window instead. ## Why Lincoln's Clay Soil Makes It Worse

Southeast Nebraska sits on heavy clay soil, and clay holds water differently than sand or loam. It soaks up moisture like a sponge, then swells when that moisture freezes. Sandy soil drains faster and moves less.

That's a problem for window wells because most of them are backfilled with whatever soil came out of the hole, not clean gravel. Water sits against the well wall, freezes, and pushes outward with real force. The same freeze-thaw cycle that might cause minor movement in sandy soil can shove a clay-backfilled well wall inches out of place over several winters.

This is also why a properly built well with a gravel drainage bed behind it holds up so much better than one that was just dropped into a dirt hole. If your well was installed years ago without gravel backfill, it's more likely to show freeze-thaw window well damage sooner than a newer, properly drained one. ## How to Protect Your Window Well From Freeze-Thaw Damage

You can't stop Nebraska winters from swinging between thaw and freeze, but you can control how much water gets into your window well in the first place. Less water in the well means less freeze-thaw pressure on the walls, cover, and frame.

- **Keep gutters and downspouts pointed away from the well.** Roof runoff dumped near a window well is one of the biggest causes of ice buildup at the base.
- **Check the grading around your foundation.** Soil should slope away from the house, not toward the well.
- **Clear leaves and debris before the first freeze.** A clogged drain bed freezes solid faster than a clean one. Our [window well drainage maintenance checklist](/blog/window-well-drainage-maintenance-checklist/) walks through a 15-minute fall routine that catches most problems early.
- **Install a well-fitted, sealed cover.** A cover that actually seats against the well rim keeps snowmelt and rain from pooling inside in the first place.
- **Inspect after the first hard freeze and again in early spring.** Damage from frost heave usually shows up right when the ground starts to thaw.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I know if my window well damage is from freeze-thaw cycles specifically?

Look for damage that lines up with the timing of winter and early spring, like new cracks appearing after a hard freeze or a wall that bows more each year. Rust at seams, cracked covers, and gaps that widen seasonally are classic freeze-thaw signs rather than one-time impact damage.

### Can freeze-thaw damage to a window well cause basement flooding?

Yes. A cracked well wall or a clogged, frozen drain forces water to pool against your foundation and basement window instead of draining away. Once spring rain and snowmelt add volume, that trapped water often finds its way through even a small gap.

### Is it cheaper to repair a window well or replace it after frost damage?

Small cracks in a cover or minor rust are usually worth repairing, often for $150 to $600. Once a wall is bowed, badly rusted, or separated from the foundation, replacement at $600 to $1,500 is typically the better long-term value since a compromised wall will keep failing every winter.

### How often should I inspect my window well for freeze-thaw damage?

Check it at least twice a year: once in late fall before the ground freezes and once in early spring as it thaws. Those two windows are when frost heave pressure is highest and when new cracks or drainage problems are easiest to spot.

Freeze-thaw window well damage rarely announces itself all at once. It shows up as a small crack, a slight lean, or water that takes a little longer to drain than it used to. Catching it in that early stage usually means a $150 to $600 fix instead of a multi-thousand-dollar rebuild.
