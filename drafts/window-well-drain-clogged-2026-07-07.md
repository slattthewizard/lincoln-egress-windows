## Why a Window Well Drain Clogged in the First Place

Most window wells built with a drain tie into your home's foundation drainage system or run to a dry well a few feet away. That pipe is usually only a few inches wide, sitting under a layer of gravel at the bottom of the well. It doesn't take much to plug it.

Leaves are the number one culprit in Lancaster County. Cottonwood fluff, maple seeds, and grass clippings wash down with every rain and settle into that gravel bed. Over a year or two, they compact into a mat that water simply can't get through. Add wind-blown dirt and the fines from eroding window well walls, and the drain opening itself can silt shut even if the pipe underneath is fine.

Nebraska's freeze-thaw cycle makes it worse. Water that doesn't drain in November sits and freezes, expanding and shifting the gravel and sometimes cracking the drain pipe connection. By spring thaw, you've got a partially collapsed drain under a well full of slush.

Older homes are more prone to this than newer ones. If your window well was installed before the 1990s, there's a real chance it was never connected to a drain at all, and what you're looking at isn't a clogged drain but a well that was designed to just soak into the ground, which stops working once the surrounding soil compacts.

## How to Clear a Clogged Window Well Drain Yourself

If you're dealing with slow drainage or a well that holds water for more than a day after rain, here's the order we'd tackle it in.

**1. Clear the surface debris first.** Pull out leaves, sticks, and loose dirt by hand or with a small garden trowel. Bag it, don't just toss it on the ground next to the well where it'll wash right back in.

**2. Dig down to the gravel.** Most wells have 4-8 inches of gravel over the drain opening. Remove it carefully with a hand trowel and set it aside in a bucket, you'll want to reuse it.

**3. Locate the drain opening.** It's typically a round or slotted opening at the base, sometimes with a short length of visible pipe. If you can't find it, that's a sign your well may never have had one installed.

**4. Clear the opening.** A plumber's snake or a stiff wire works for loosening compacted debris right at the opening. For water that's already standing, a wet/dry shop vac with a hose attachment will pull both the water and the muck out in one pass.

**5. Flush it.** Once it looks clear, run a garden hose into the well and watch how fast the water level drops. It should visibly go down within a few minutes. If it doesn't move at all, the clog is deeper in the pipe than you can reach by hand.

**6. Re-bed the gravel.** Put the gravel back in, keeping it loose rather than packed down, so water can move through it freely.

This is a job most homeowners can do themselves in under an hour with tools they already own. Where it gets past DIY territory is when the drain line is crushed, disconnected from the foundation drain tile, or buried deeper than you can reach with a trowel. That's when you need someone who can run a camera down the line or dig it up properly.

## When It's Not the Drain: Grading and Cover Problems

Sometimes what looks like a clogged drain is actually a grading or cover issue, and clearing the pipe won't fix it.

If the ground around your window well slopes toward the house instead of away from it, water pours into the well faster than any drain can handle, clogged or not. This is common on older Lincoln lots where landscaping has settled over decades. Regrading a few feet around the well, even just adding and compacting soil to create a slight slope away from the foundation, can cut the volume of water hitting the well by more than half.

A missing or damaged window well cover is the other big one. Covers keep out the leaves and dirt that cause clogs to begin with, and they also stop the well from filling like a rain barrel during a downpour. If your cover is cracked, sagging, or missing altogether, you're fighting the clog problem over and over instead of preventing it. Covers and ladders typically run $150-$600 installed depending on well size and material.

## Preventing the Next Clog

A little maintenance twice a year keeps this from becoming a recurring headache.

**Spring:** Clear out winter debris and check that the drain flows freely before the heavy spring rains hit. This is also the time to check your cover for cracks from freeze-thaw.

**Fall:** Clear leaves before they mat down and freeze in place. If you've got trees near your window wells, a simple mesh screen under the cover can cut leaf buildup dramatically.

**After any heavy rain:** Take thirty seconds to glance at your window wells. Standing water that doesn't drain within a day is your early warning sign, long before it becomes a flooded basement.

## When to Call a Professional

Some situations are worth handing off rather than tackling yourself.

Call a pro if the drain line runs under concrete, a patio, or a walkway, since that's excavation and cutting work, not a weekend project. Call a pro if you've cleared the visible opening and water still won't drain, because the blockage or break is likely further down the line than a hand tool can reach. And call a pro anytime the fix would mean digging near buried electrical, gas lines, or your home's foundation drain tile system, where a mistake gets expensive fast.

There's also the legal angle to keep in mind. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I know if my window well drain is clogged versus just missing entirely?

Dig down through the gravel at the bottom of the well and look for a pipe opening. If you find one that's full of dirt or debris, it's clogged and usually cleanable. If you don't find any opening or pipe at all, the well was likely never connected to a drain system, which is common in homes built before the 1990s.

### Can I pour anything down the drain to unclog it, like a plumbing product?

We don't recommend chemical drain cleaners for window well drains. They're designed for indoor plumbing, not gravel-and-soil drainage systems, and won't reach a clog that's packed into the pipe or the ground around it. A shop vac, plumber's snake, and a garden hose flush are safer and more effective.

### How much does it cost to fix a window well drain in Lincoln?

A simple debris clear-out is often free if you do it yourself, or $150-$300 if you have us do it. A full drain repair or new drain tie-in typically runs $600-$1,500 depending on how much excavation is needed. If the well itself is rusted or damaged, a full window well replacement is usually $600-$1,500 as well.

### Will a clogged window well drain cause basement flooding?

It can. Water that can't drain out of the well will eventually find the path of least resistance, which is often the seal around your basement window. Over time this leads to leaks, and in freeze-thaw conditions it can also put pressure on the well wall itself. Clearing the drain early is a lot cheaper than dealing with basement water damage later.
