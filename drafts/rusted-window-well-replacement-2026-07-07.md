# Rusted Window Well Replacement: When to Repair and When to Replace

Push your thumb against the side of your window well and does it crumble like a stale cracker? That flaking, orange-brown metal is telling you something, and it's not just about looks. A rusted window well can still be structurally sound, or it can be one hard rain away from collapsing into your basement window.

The good news is that you don't have to guess. There's a clear line between a rusted window well that just needs a repair and one that needs a full rusted window well replacement, and most homeowners in Lincoln can figure out which side of that line they're on in about ten minutes.

This guide walks through how to tell the difference, what each option actually costs, and when rust becomes a code or safety problem instead of just an eyesore.

## How Bad Is the Rust? The First Thing to Check

Not all rust is equal. Surface rust that formed a light film on galvanized steel is annoying, but it's usually cosmetic. Deep rust that's eaten through the metal, left holes you can see daylight through, or caused the well to separate from the foundation wall is a different story.

Walk your window well and check for these signs:

- Flaking or bubbling paint with orange staining underneath
- Soft spots you can push a screwdriver through
- Gaps where the well has pulled away from the house
- Dirt or gravel washing into the basement window frame
- A well that visibly leans or bulges outward

If you're only seeing the first item on that list, a repair is often on the table. If you're seeing three or more, you're probably looking at a rusted window well replacement, and it's worth getting a second opinion before the next big rain. ## When a Repair Still Makes Sense

If the rust is surface-level and the well is still bolted tight to the foundation, a repair can buy you several more years. That usually means wire-brushing the affected area, treating it with a rust-inhibiting primer, and recoating or bracing the weak spots.

Dan Kowalski in Waverly called us after noticing rust streaks on his window well liner during spring cleanup. The steel was still solid underneath, just corroded on the surface from years of standing water at the base. Our crew cleared out the old gravel bed, treated the rust, and reset the drainage rock for $420. No new well needed, and the fix has held up through two winters.

Repair is the right call when:

- The rust is confined to one section, not the whole well
- The well is still securely anchored to the foundation
- There's no visible separation or bowing
- The window and frame behind it are dry and undamaged
- You checked it last season and the rust hasn't visibly spread

A quick way to track this yourself is to snap a phone photo of the worst spot every spring. If the rusted area looks the same size a year later, a repair is probably still holding. If it's noticeably bigger, that's your sign the clock is running out on patch jobs.

This is also a good time to fix the root cause. Most rust starts because water is sitting against the metal instead of draining away. If your well fills up after every storm, patching the rust without fixing the drainage just means you'll be back here in two years. ## When Rusted Window Well Replacement Is the Smarter Call

Once rust has eaten through the metal in more than a spot or two, patching stops being a repair and starts being a stall tactic. Corrugated steel wells corrode from the inside out, so by the time you see a hole, the surrounding metal is usually weaker than it looks.

Maria Lopez in Beatrice had a corrugated steel well that was original to her 1970s house. It had thinned out enough that soil was pushing through small holes near the base every time it rained, and the whole structure had started to lean. A new polymer well, sized to code and properly backfilled with gravel, ran $980 installed. She hasn't had a drainage complaint since.

Replacement is the right call when:

- Rust has created actual holes, not just surface staining
- The well has pulled away from the foundation or is visibly leaning
- You're dealing with repeated flooding or soil intrusion
- The well is more than 20-25 years old and made of thin corrugated steel

This is also the moment to reconsider materials. Steel wells rust because they're steel. A polymer or fiberglass well won't have this problem again, and pricing is close enough that most homeowners just make the switch. ## What Happens If You Ignore a Rusted Window Well

Rust doesn't stay put. Left alone, it keeps thinning the metal until the well can't hold back the soil around it anymore. That's when small problems turn into expensive ones.

In Crete, the Petersons noticed a little rust on their window well two summers ago and figured it could wait. By this spring, the corroded section had given way under saturated soil after a heavy May storm, and dirt had pushed against the basement window frame hard enough to crack the seal. What could have been a $700 well swap turned into a $2,450 job that included a new well, a repaired window frame, and cleanup for water that got into the finished basement wall.

Beyond the cost, there's a safety angle. A window well is part of your home's emergency escape route if it serves a bedroom. A well that's collapsing or filling with debris can block that exit when it matters most.

It's also worth knowing that the myth about rust itself causing tetanus isn't accurate. The [CDC notes that tetanus bacteria live in soil and dust](https://www.cdc.gov/tetanus/causes/index.html), not in rust, but a jagged rusted edge is still a real cut hazard. Handle any teardown carefully, or let a crew with the right gloves and tools do it.

If you're chipping away at a badly rusted well and hit a spot where it's bolted into concrete or where the foundation itself looks damaged, stop. Cutting into concrete, dealing with structural cracks, or working near any wiring that runs close to a window well is professional territory, not a weekend project.

## How to Choose a Material That Won't Rust Again

If you're replacing a rusted well anyway, it's worth spending a few extra minutes on material choice so you're not back here in another decade.

- **Galvanized steel** is the cheapest option and holds up fine if drainage is good, but it's also what rusted the first time.
- **Polymer or plastic wells** cost slightly more but never rust, and they're the most common upgrade we install now.
- **Corrugated aluminum** resists rust better than steel and costs less than polymer, though it can dent more easily.

Whatever material you pick, the well is only as good as the drainage underneath it. A gravel bed with a working drain tile connection keeps water moving away from the metal instead of sitting against it and starting the corrosion cycle over again. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can a rusted window well be repaired instead of replaced?

Yes, if the rust is surface-level and the well is still solidly attached to the foundation. Wire-brushing, rust-inhibiting primer, and fixing the drainage underneath can add several years of life for a few hundred dollars. Once you see actual holes or the well pulling away from the house, repair usually isn't worth it anymore.

### How much does rusted window well replacement cost in Lincoln?

Most straightforward window well replacements run $600-$1,500 depending on size and material. If the egress window itself needs replacing too, add $1,200-$3,000 for a swap in an existing opening. A permit, when required, typically costs $75-$200.

### How long does a window well last before it rusts out?

Galvanized steel wells commonly last 15-25 years before rust becomes a real structural issue, though poor drainage can cut that timeline in half. Polymer and fiberglass wells don't rust at all, which is why a lot of homeowners switch materials when it's time to replace.

### Is a rusted window well a safety issue, not just a cosmetic one?

It can be. If the well serves a bedroom egress window, a collapsing or blocked well can interfere with your emergency escape route. A badly rusted well can also let soil and water push against the window frame, which leads to cracked seals and basement leaks over time.

Rust in a window well isn't a reason to panic, but it's not something to put off indefinitely either.
