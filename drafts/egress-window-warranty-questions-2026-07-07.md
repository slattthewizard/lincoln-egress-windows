## Why Warranty Questions Matter Before You Sign

An egress window installation touches three separate systems: the window unit itself, the labor that cuts the opening and sets the frame, and the well and drainage that keep water out of your basement. Each of those has its own failure points, and often its own warranty. If you only ask about "the warranty" as a single blanket idea, you'll miss gaps that matter.

## The Three Types of Egress Window Warranties

Egress window projects typically involve three distinct warranty categories. Understanding the difference is the foundation for asking good egress window installation warranty questions.

**Manufacturer warranty** covers the physical window unit itself, meaning the frame, glass, hardware, and seals as they were built at the factory. This warranty follows the product, not the installer, and it protects against defects like a cracked frame, failed insulated glass, or hardware that breaks under normal use.

**Workmanship warranty** covers the labor and installation quality, meaning how the window was set into the opening, how it was flashed, sealed, and secured. This is the warranty that protects you if a properly manufactured window was installed incorrectly and starts leaking, sticking, or settling.

**Waterproofing warranty** covers the window well, drainage, and any waterproofing membrane or gravel bed installed around the opening. Basement window projects almost always involve grading and drainage work, and that work has its own failure modes that are separate from the window unit or the frame installation.

| Warranty Type | What It Covers | What It Does NOT Cover |
|---|---|---|
| Manufacturer | Frame, glass, hardware defects from the factory | Installation errors, well drainage, water intrusion from grading |
| Workmanship | Labor quality: sealing, flashing, frame setting, alignment | Product defects, well wall failure, drainage backups |
| Waterproofing | Window well, drainage gravel, membrane, grading around the opening | The window unit itself, hardware issues, glass defects |

Ask your contractor to identify, in writing, which category applies to each part of the job. If they can't separate the three clearly, that's a sign the paperwork you'll receive later won't be clear either.

## Warranty Questions to Ask About Materials and Manufacturer Coverage

The manufacturer side of the equation is usually the most straightforward, but it still deserves specific egress window installation warranty questions rather than a general "yes, it's covered."

Ask what the manufacturer warranty covers item by item: frame material, glass unit (including seal failure and fogging between panes), and hardware like hinges, locks, and cranks. Vinyl and fiberglass frames typically hold up differently than older wood-framed units, and coverage terms can vary by material. Finally, ask for the manufacturer's documentation in writing, not just a verbal summary. A reputable installer will hand you the actual warranty card or document, not a paraphrase.

## Warranty Questions to Ask About Workmanship and Labor

Workmanship coverage is where a lot of the real risk sits, because it's the part most likely to be vague or missing altogether from a low-cost bid. This is exactly where you want to slow down and ask direct egress window installation warranty questions before signing.

Ask how long the workmanship warranty lasts. There's no single industry standard, and terms vary a lot between contractors, so get the specific number in writing rather than assuming "it's covered." Compare that number across every bid you collect.

Ask what happens if a leak, gap, or seal failure shows up after the workmanship warranty period ends. Some contractors offer discounted repair rates for past-warranty issues; others charge full price. Knowing this in advance helps you budget realistically.

## Waterproofing and Window Well Warranty Questions

Water is the number one enemy of any basement egress project, and waterproofing warranties tend to be the most misunderstood part of the whole package. Ask specifically what's covered here, separate from the window and the labor.

Ask whether the warranty covers the window well itself (rust, cracking, or wall failure), the drainage system beneath and around it, or both. This is worth pinning down before you commit to any [window well installation](/window-well-installation-lincoln/), since well and drainage warranties are often separate from the window warranty entirely. These are different failure points. A well can be structurally sound while the drainage underneath clogs or was never properly graded. Our guide on [how window well drainage works](/blog/how-window-well-drainage-works/) explains the mechanics if you want the full picture.

Ask how flooding events are handled. Nebraska's freeze-thaw cycles and spring rains put real stress on window wells, and a warranty that excludes "acts of nature" or heavy rainfall may leave you with very little actual protection. A resident in Seward found this out when a well filled with water during a heavy spring storm and the waterproofing warranty excluded rainfall events entirely, leaving them to cover a $700 drainage correction themselves.

Ask whether the warranty requires any maintenance on your part, like keeping the well clear of leaves and debris, to stay valid. Some waterproofing warranties are void if the drain gets clogged from lack of maintenance. Our [window well drainage maintenance checklist](/blog/window-well-drainage-maintenance-checklist/) is a quick reference for staying on the right side of that requirement.

Ask what the warranty says about window well wall bowing or shifting over time, since soil pressure and freeze-thaw cycles are common causes in this region. Structural issues with the well wall are a different repair than a simple drainage fix, and cost more to correct.

## Red Flags: When a Warranty Isn't Worth the Paper It's Printed On

Not every warranty offered is worth much. A few patterns should make you pause before signing.

Watch for warranties that exist only verbally or as a single vague line in the contract, like "workmanship guaranteed." Ask for the specific duration, what's excluded, and who to call if something goes wrong. If the salesperson can't produce that in writing on request, treat it as a red flag.

Watch for warranty language that's transferable to the manufacturer only, with no separate workmanship or waterproofing coverage mentioned at all. That leaves you exposed on exactly the two areas (labor and drainage) most likely to cause problems.

Watch for pressure to sign quickly "before the price goes up." A legitimate contractor will give you time to review warranty terms and compare bids. Rushing you past the paperwork is a tactic, not a courtesy.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What's the difference between a manufacturer warranty and a workmanship warranty on an egress window?

A manufacturer warranty covers defects in the physical window unit itself, like the frame, glass, or hardware. A workmanship warranty covers the quality of the installation labor, meaning how the window was set, sealed, and flashed into the opening. A leak from bad sealing usually falls under workmanship, not the manufacturer, even though the water is coming through the window.

### Should I get warranty terms in writing before signing an egress window contract?

Yes, always. Verbal promises about warranty coverage aren't enforceable if a dispute comes up later. Ask for the specific warranty duration, what's covered, what's excluded, and who to contact for claims, all spelled out in the written contract or a separate warranty document you keep on file.

### Does a window well warranty cover flooding after heavy rain?

It depends on the contractor and the specific terms, so this is one of the most important egress window installation warranty questions to ask directly. Some waterproofing warranties cover drainage failures but exclude major rainfall events or acts of nature. Ask for this in plain language before you sign, since Nebraska's spring storms make it a realistic scenario.

### Can a warranty be voided if I don't maintain the window well myself?

In many cases, yes. Some waterproofing and drainage warranties require basic upkeep, like clearing leaves and debris from the well and keeping the drain unobstructed, to remain valid. Ask your contractor exactly what maintenance is required on your end and how often, so you don't accidentally void coverage you're paying for.
