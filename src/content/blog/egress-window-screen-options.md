## Why Regular Window Screens Don't Belong on an Egress Window

A standard aluminum screen from a hardware store is built for a double-hung window on the second floor. It's meant to keep flies out, not to survive being kicked open by a panicked ten-year-old at 2 a.m. Most off-the-shelf screens use thin push-pin tabs that jam or snap when force is applied quickly.

The International Residential Code doesn't regulate screens directly, but it does require the window itself to open without keys, tools, or special knowledge. If the screen frame blocks that operation or has to be removed with a screwdriver, the window fails inspection even if the sash itself meets the size and clearance rules. The screen has to come off, slide out of the way, or hinge open in one motion.

The U.S. Consumer Product Safety Commission also warns that ordinary screens were never designed to hold a person's weight or stop a fall, so they shouldn't be treated as a safety barrier on their own (see the CPSC's [guide to preventing window falls](https://www.cpsc.gov/safety-education/safety-guides/home/preventing-window-falls)). For an egress window, the screen's job is airflow and bug control, nothing more. Anything that adds resistance to opening the window defeats the purpose.

## The Egress Window Screen Options Lincoln Homeowners Actually Use

There are really four egress window screen options we install or repair regularly around Lancaster, Seward, Saline, and Gage counties.

**Fold-out or hinged mesh screens** attach to the interior or exterior frame and swing open with the sash. They're the simplest fix for casement and awning-style egress windows because the screen moves with the window instead of blocking it.

**Sliding track screens** ride in a channel alongside a slider or single-hung egress window. They push aside in one motion, which keeps the full opening clear the moment someone needs out.

**Security or impact screens** use a heavier gauge mesh, sometimes with a reinforced frame, and still release from the inside without tools. These cost more but hold up better against hail and curious pets, and they're a common request from Hickman and Waverly homeowners who back up to open fields.

**Retractable roll screens** tuck into a small housing above or beside the window and pull down only when needed. They're the least common option in a basement setting but work well when the window well is shallow and a fixed frame would stick out too far.

Every one of these has to pass the same test: can someone inside the room open the window and clear the screen in a single, obvious motion, in the dark, without a key.

## Window Well Screens vs. Egress Window Screens: You Might Need Both

Here's where people get confused. A window well screen or cover sits over the top of the window well itself, keeping leaves, rain, and small animals from collecting down at the window. An egress window screen is mounted on the window unit and controls what comes through the glass opening.

You can have one without the other, but most basement bedrooms in Southeast Nebraska end up needing both. The well cover keeps the well from turning into a swamp every spring, and the window screen keeps mosquitoes out on the nights you actually crack the window for air.

## DIY Screen Swaps vs. Calling a Pro

Swapping a basic fold-out or sliding screen on a window you already own is a reasonable weekend project for most homeowners. You'll need the correct frame size, the right mesh gauge, and a few minutes with a screwdriver or trim tool.

Where it stops being a DIY job:

- The window well needs to be excavated or reshaped to fit a new screen or cover system.
- The frame around the window is rusted, rotted, or pulling away from the foundation.
- You're not sure whether the current window even meets Lincoln's egress size and clearance rules.
- The screen problem is really a drainage problem, with standing water or mold near the frame.

Any of those point to a bigger issue than the screen itself, and cutting corners on a basement exit isn't worth the risk to your family or your resale value. ## Frequently Asked Questions

### Do egress window screens have to meet a specific code requirement?

The code focuses on the window opening without tools, keys, or special knowledge, not the screen material itself. In practice, that means any screen you use has to release in a single, obvious motion. If a screen requires unscrewing a frame or finding a hidden latch, it won't pass inspection even if the window underneath is sized correctly.

### Can I install a security screen on an egress window myself?

Basic fold-out or sliding screens are usually a manageable DIY swap if the window frame and well are in good shape. Security or impact screens have heavier hardware and need to be mounted so they still release quickly from inside, which is worth having checked by a professional the first time.

### Will a window well cover stop leaves and rain from reaching my egress window screen?

A well-fitted cover blocks most debris and slows down water intake, which protects the screen and frame from swelling or rusting. It won't replace proper drainage, though, so a clogged well drain can still cause problems even with a good cover installed.

### How much does it cost to replace a torn or stuck egress window screen in Lincoln?

Most standard mesh screen replacements run $120 to $300 installed, while security or retractable screens run higher, up to $500. If the window well or frame needs repair at the same time, that adds to the total, which is why it's worth having a professional check the whole opening rather than just swapping the screen.
