## Why Every Basement Bedroom Buyer Needs an Egress Checklist

A basement bedroom isn't legally a bedroom unless it has a code-compliant emergency escape and rescue opening, commonly called an egress window. Nebraska and the City of Lincoln both require it in any basement sleeping room, and Lincoln's code extends that rule to any basement room with a closet at least 18 inches deep or direct bathroom access, whether or not the listing calls it a "bedroom."

That distinction matters for three reasons. First, it affects the legal bedroom count, which affects the home's appraised value and what you can actually claim it's worth later. Second, most mortgage appraisals will only count a basement room as a bedroom if it has proper egress, which can change your loan numbers mid-transaction. Third, and most simply, it's a safety issue for whoever sleeps down there.

Sellers don't always know their basement bedroom is non-compliant, and some who do know hope nobody asks. This article is written for you, the buyer, and what to check before you're the one holding the problem.

## The Basement Bedroom Egress Checklist: What to Check Before You Close

Walk into the basement bedroom and check these items yourself before your inspector even shows up. None of it requires tools.

- **Is there a window at all?** A basement bedroom with no exterior window fails egress automatically, full stop, no matter how big or nice the room is.
- **Can the window actually open?** Painted-shut, swollen, or rusted-stuck windows don't count as usable egress even if they're the right size. Try it yourself if the seller allows access.
- **Is the opening big enough?** Nebraska's adopted code requires a net clear opening of at least 5.7 square feet, with a minimum opening height of 24 inches and a minimum width of 20 inches. A tape measure settles this in about 30 seconds.
- **Is the sill height workable?** The bottom of the clear opening can't sit more than 44 inches above the finished floor. Anything higher usually means the window itself needs to move, not just get replaced.
- **Is there a window well outside, and is it deep enough?** If the window sits below grade, code requires a window well with enough horizontal projection to let the window open fully, plus a permanently fixed ladder if the well is deeper than 44 inches.
- **Does the well drain, or does it hold water?** Standing water, rust stains, or a bowed well wall are signs of a drainage problem that a home inspector often misses if it's dry on inspection day.
- **Is there a permit on file?** Lincoln's building department can usually confirm whether the basement egress work was permitted. No permit is a real yellow flag, not necessarily a dealbreaker.

If you check every box and something still looks off, get the window measured by someone who does this daily rather than guessing. ## How to Negotiate Egress Repairs Into Your Purchase Contract

Once you've identified an egress problem, you have three real options, and they're not equally good.

**Ask for a price reduction.** This works well when you'd rather control the project yourself and pick your own contractor and timeline after closing. It also avoids delaying the closing date while repairs happen.

**Ask for a repair credit at closing.** A credit is often cleaner than a price reduction for financing purposes, since some lenders treat seller credits differently than a straight price cut. Get a written estimate first so the credit amount reflects real numbers, not a guess.

**Ask the seller to complete the repair before closing.** This only makes sense if you have time to spare and can verify the work is done correctly and permitted, since a rushed pre-closing repair is where corners tend to get cut.

Whichever route you pick, get the scope in writing and attach the estimate to your inspection response. A vague repair request tends to produce a vague repair. ## Home Inspection Red Flags That Point to an Egress Problem

Standard home inspections cover a lot of ground, and egress windows sometimes get less attention than the furnace or the roof. Watch for these signs even if your inspector doesn't flag them directly.

A window that's painted, caulked, or bolted shut is an immediate concern, since it can't function as an emergency exit regardless of size. Rust streaks or efflorescence, the white chalky residue on concrete, around a window well usually point to a drainage issue that's been going on for a while. A window well cover that's cracked, warped, or missing entirely is both a safety hazard and a sign of deferred maintenance.

Musty basement smell near the window area often means water has been getting in around the well for longer than the seller has mentioned. And if the basement room has a closet or its own bathroom but no window at all, that's the scenario Lincoln's code specifically targets, even if the listing avoids the word "bedroom." You can review the City of Lincoln's own building and permit requirements directly at [Building and Safety, City of Lincoln](https://www.lincoln.ne.gov/city/build/residential.htm).

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I know if a basement bedroom has legal egress before buying?

Measure the window opening yourself. It needs a net clear opening of at least 5.7 square feet, a minimum height of 24 inches, a minimum width of 20 inches, and a sill no more than 44 inches above the floor. If it's below grade, there also needs to be a code-size window well, with a ladder if the well is deeper than 44 inches.

### Can I back out of a purchase if the basement bedroom isn't egress compliant?

That depends on your contract's inspection contingency, which typically gives you the right to request repairs, a credit, or a price reduction, and in many cases to walk away if the seller won't negotiate. Talk to your real estate agent or attorney about your specific contract language. The [CFPB's guide to home inspections](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/owning-a-home/process/close/inspection/) explains how inspection contingencies generally work.

### Does a non-compliant basement bedroom affect my mortgage or appraisal?

It can. Appraisers are supposed to count only legally compliant rooms as bedrooms, so if a basement room lacks egress, the appraised value and the listed bedroom count can shift once that's flagged. This sometimes surfaces during underwriting even if it wasn't caught in the initial listing.

### How much does it typically cost to fix a basement bedroom's egress window?

It ranges widely based on what's actually wrong. A stuck window or damaged well can run $600 to $3,000, while a basement with no opening at all usually needs a full cut-in installation between $3,500 and $9,500 depending on the foundation and finishing involved.
